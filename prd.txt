Executive Summary
MediScan AI is an explainable artificial intelligence platform designed to serve as a
“Second Opinion as a Service” for radiologists and clinicians analyzing chest X-rays.
Rather than replacing clinical judgment, MediScan AI reduces radiologist burnout by
automating the detection of common pathologies—particularly pneumonia—and
providing interpretable visual explanations through Grad-CAM heatmaps. The
platform emphasizes model transparency and clinical usability, enabling healthcare
professionals to understand why the model made a specific prediction before making
diagnostic decisions.
The core value proposition is simple: Upload → Analyze → Visualize → Report. In a 6-
hour hackathon environment, the focus is entirely on model consumption (using pre-
trained weights) and user experience, avoiding the computational overhead of training
custom models.
1. Problem Statement
1.1 Clinical Context
Chest X-ray interpretation is one of the most frequently performed diagnostic
procedures globally, with an estimated 2 billion chest radiographs acquired annually
[1]. However, radiologists face significant challenges:
Cognitive Overload: Radiologists interpret hundreds of images daily, leading to
fatigue and increased diagnostic error rates [2].
Diagnostic Variability: Inter-observer agreement for pneumonia detection
ranges from 70–85%, depending on severity and radiologist experience [3].
Burnout Crisis: The radiology workforce shortage is exacerbated by repetitive,
high-volume workloads [4].
Accessibility Gap: In resource-limited settings, trained radiologists are scarce,
delaying diagnosis and treatment [5].
1.2 Existing Solutions & Limitations
Current commercial solutions (e.g., IBM Watson, GE Healthcare AI) either:
Lack interpretability, making clinicians hesitant to trust predictions (“black box”
problem)
Require extensive fine-tuning on proprietary datasets, increasing deployment
time
Are prohibitively expensive for small clinics or research institutions
Provide confidence scores without visual localization, limiting clinical utility
1.3 The Opportunity
A lightweight, explainable, and rapidly deployable AI system can:
1. Highlight regions of interest in seconds, reducing time-to-diagnosis
2. Provide confidence scores for multiple pathologies (pneumonia, effusion, etc.)
3. Generate structured clinical reports for integration into existing EHR systems
4. Serve as a triage tool to prioritize urgent cases
2. Proposed Solution
2.1 Product Vision
MediScan AI is a web-based clinical decision support system that:
1. Accepts chest X-ray images (JPEG/PNG) via intuitive drag-and-drop interface
2. Analyzes images using a pre-trained deep learning model in real-time
3. Visualizes model predictions with Grad-CAM heatmaps showing areas of
diagnostic interest
4. Reports findings with confidence scores, doctor’s notes, and downloadable PDF
summaries

3.2 Inference Pipeline
Input X-ray (JPEG/PNG/DICOM)
    ↓
[Gatekeeper] Image Quality Check: Valid AP Chest X-ray? (if not, reject with "Invalid Scan")
    ↓
[Preprocessing] Resize to 224×224, normalize (ImageNet stats) (For DICOM, extract pixel array first)
    ↓
[Model Inference] Forward pass through ResNet50/DenseNet121
    ↓
[Output] Logits → Softmax → Confidence scores (0–100%)
    ↓
[Confidence Floor] If max probability < 70%, disable Grad-CAM. Flag for "Uncertain clinical presentation. Manual review required."
    ↓
[Grad-CAM] Compute gradients w.r.t. last conv layer (only for >70% confidence)
    ↓
[Heatmap] Generate attention map; overlay on original image
    ↓
[Report] Combine image, heatmap, scores, notes → PDF
    ↓
[Smart Triage] Re-order pending worklist, pushing urgent cases (e.g., P(Pneumonia) > 0.90) to the top.
3.3 Grad-CAM for Explainability
Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) is a technique that
produces visual explanations for predictions by computing the gradient of the target
class with respect to the final convolutional layer [6]:
Where:
 = activation maps from the final convolutional layer
 = gradient weights (average pooled gradients)
 = target class (e.g., “Pneumonia”)
Clinical Interpretation: The resulting heatmap highlights regions the model considers
most relevant for its prediction. Red regions indicate high model confidence; blue
regions indicate low confidence. This allows clinicians to verify whether the model is
focusing on clinically meaningful features (e.g., consolidation patterns) or artifacts.

To prevent Grad-CAM hallucinations (e.g., focusing on "L"/"R" markers, pacemakers, or collarbones) and mitigate diagnosis liability, a "Confidence Floor" is implemented:
If the model's highest predicted probability is under 70%, the Grad-CAM visualization is disabled entirely. A warning is displayed: "Uncertain clinical presentation. Manual radiologist review required." Acknowledging uncertainty builds trust and avoids the clinical/legal risks of false "diagnosis."

Appendix B: Sample Workflow
Scenario: Dr. Sarah, an emergency physician, uses MediScan AI to triage a suspected
pneumonia case.
1. Upload: Dr. Sarah drags a chest X-ray into the browser
2. Analysis: System processes image in second; displays heatmap highlighting right
lower lobe consolidation
3. Confidence: Bar chart shows Pneumonia: 92%, Normal: 7%, Effusion: 1%
4. Notes: Dr. Sarah adds: “Clinical presentation consistent with bacterial
pneumonia; recommend antibiotics”
5. Report: Clicks “Download PDF”; receives report with image, heatmap, scores,
and notes
6. Action: Forwards report to attending radiologist for confirmation; initiates
